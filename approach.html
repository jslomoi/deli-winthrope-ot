How I work

Sessions that start where your child is.

No preset programme. No fixed agenda. We figure it out together, starting with what's hardest.

Every child I work with is different. That sounds obvious, but it's worth saying: I don't run programmes. I don't have a six-week curriculum I deliver to every family. I start by trying to understand what's actually hard for your child, and what's already working.

The approach I use draws mostly on Ayres Sensory Integration® and DIR/Floortime. Both prioritise following the child's lead, building from the inside out, and trusting that regulation comes before behaviour change. I also draw on the Zones of Regulation framework for older children who are ready to build some metacognitive awareness around how their body feels.

In practice, that means: a lot of floor time, a lot of careful observation, and a lot of conversations with you about what you're seeing at home and at school.

Session one

The first session is mostly listening.

The first session is mostly listening: to you, and to your child if they're up for it. We don't push. We sit on the floor, build something out of magnatiles, talk about what mornings look like in your house. By the end, I'll have a sense of where to start, and we'll agree on what the next two or three sessions could focus on.

You're welcome to stay for the whole first session. Some parents prefer to be in the room; others prefer to wait outside. There's no right answer. I'll follow your lead and your child's lead.

What to bring

  • Any recent reports (OT, speech, paediatric, school)
  • A list of the things that are hardest right now (written down is fine)
  • Your child, dressed comfortably (they'll probably want to move)
  • A snack if your child needs one
Duration

60–75 minutes

Communication

Reports & how I keep you in the loop

After every session I send a brief email, usually 3 to 5 sentences, covering what we worked on and one or two things to try at home. Not a formal report. Just enough that you know what happened and aren't starting from scratch the next morning.

Formal progress reports are written every 6–12 months, or when needed for NDIS plan reviews, school funding applications, or paediatric referrals. These are thorough: they summarise assessment findings, progress against goals, and recommendations. I write them in plain English, with a clinical summary at the back for the professionals who need that level of detail.

If your school wants to talk to me directly, I'm happy to join a meeting or a phone call. That kind of communication isn't extra. It's part of the work.

Honest commitments

What I won't do

This section exists because I think it matters to say these things out loud.

I won't promise outcomes.

Not every child lands in the same place. Most leave more settled than they came. But I won't tell you your child will be regulated by week eight, because I don't know that, and neither does anyone else.

I won't recommend therapy your child doesn't need.

If three sessions in I think your child is doing well and doesn't need weekly OT, I'll say so. I'd rather lose a client than keep a family coming unnecessarily.

I won't ignore what your gut is telling you.

You've been watching your child since before they could talk. If something feels off, I want to hear it. Even if I disagree with your read, your gut is data, and I'll treat it that way.

I won't use jargon without explaining it.

If I use a clinical term and your face goes blank, please stop me. My job is to translate the complexity of what's happening in your child's nervous system into something you can actually use at 7:30am.

I won't make your child feel like a problem to be fixed.

They're not. Their nervous system is doing exactly what it evolved to do. It just needs a bit more support to find the steady. That's what we're building toward.

Common questions

FAQ

What does the first session look like?
Mostly listening: to you, and to your child if they're up for it. We don't push. We sit on the floor, build something out of magnatiles, and talk about what mornings look like in your house. By the end, I'll have a sense of where to start and we'll agree together on the next steps.
How often will sessions happen?
Most families start fortnightly, sometimes weekly for the first six weeks if the need is acute. We taper as your child builds capacity. We'll agree on cadence together at the end of the first session. Nothing is locked in.
Should I stay in the room with my child?
That's entirely up to you and your child. Some kids do better with a parent present. Some find it easier to engage when they know mum or dad is just in the waiting area. We'll read the room together and you can always change your mind session to session.
How long will we need to come?
It depends. Some families come for eight sessions and feel like they have enough to work with independently. Others come monthly for two years because the ongoing relationship is useful. I don't have a fixed length. I'll tell you honestly when I think sessions are no longer the best use of your time and money.
My child has never done OT before. Will it be overwhelming?
I'll pace everything to what your child can handle. The first session is low-demand: no formal assessments, no structured tasks. We just hang out and I watch how they move and play. It rarely feels clinical to the kids, which is the point.
Can you work with my child's school directly?
Yes. With your consent, I can attend school meetings, write letters to class teachers, and provide school-based consultation visits. Some of this is billable as an NDIS support item; I'll be clear about what's what before we proceed.
Do you do formal assessments?
Yes. I conduct functional capacity assessments (FCA) for NDIS plan reviews, and I can administer standardised tools including the Sensory Processing Measure (SPM) and the Beery VMI when appropriate. These require a longer session and produce a formal written report.
What if things aren't working?
Tell me. I'd rather hear it directly than have you quietly stop booking sessions. If an approach isn't landing, we change it. If I'm not the right fit, I'll do my best to refer you somewhere that is. The goal is your child feeling steadier, not my ego.
